Proposal 1: Animated Adaptation of 'Kwa Atta, Gyae Anibre' for Adult Audiences

What I am proposing to do:

In Ghana, animation is dismissed as children's entertainment. When Hawk and Hen, my final year thesis project, premiered at Ghana Animators Week 2024, adults who normally ignore animation engaged with it because I used Ghanaian oral storytelling structures like call-and-response pacing, familiar archetypes, and proverb-based conflict resolution. This showed me the stigma isn't about animation itself but about how we structure stories within it.

I will create a 3-4 minute animated adaptation of Nana Kwame Ampadu's highlife narrative "Kwa Atta, Gyae Anibre" (Kwa Atta, Stop Being Jealous). The story follows two hunters - Dapaah, who is generous and receives wealth from forest dwarfs, and Kwa Atta, whose greed leads to his hand being trapped in a cursed sack and amputated. This tale is embedded in Ghanaian adult consciousness through Ampadu's music, making it ideal for testing whether animation can carry the same weight as the original song.

I will structure the film using specific oral techniques: musical narration following Ampadu's call-and-response structure, parallel journeys with repetitive-but-varied sequences as both hunters meet the dwarf king, and visual proverbs contrasting light and darkness when the sacks open. The dwarf king will function as narrator and character, directly addressing the audience like a griot.

What I hope to learn:

Which oral storytelling techniques translate effectively from Ampadu's song format to animated form and which require adaptation? Does the musical narration need to drive every scene or can animation carry meaning visually between musical moments? How do design choices for the dwarf characters affect whether the story reads as folktale versus children's fantasy in Ghanaian context? I will document these production decisions to understand what makes animated adaptations of existing oral narratives feel legitimate to adult audiences, creating a framework other Ghanaian animators can reference when adapting cultural material.

Proposal 2: Cultural Specificity in Character Animation

What I am proposing to do:

Available 3D character assets lack cultural specificity. Generic models don't capture the distinct movement patterns, gestural language, body proportions, and environmental interactions that ground animated characters in particular cultural contexts. When animators adapt these generic assets for their local audiences, characters feel disconnected from the cultural worlds they inhabit, which contributes to audiences dismissing animation as unconvincing.

I will investigate whether culturally specific character assets affect audience perception of animation authenticity. Using photogrammetry, sculpting and modelling techniques to capture unique Ghanaian physical features, movement patterns, and gestural language, I will create production-ready character assets that preserve cultural distinctness while being stylized for animation. I will create two versions of the same 2-3 minute animated sequence: one using these culturally observed Ghanaian assets, another using adapted generic models. The process of creating both versions, alongside feedback from peers and faculty at IADT, will inform my understanding of which cultural elements translate visually and which are lost in generic adaptation.

What I hope to learn:

Does cultural specificity in character animation affect perception across different audiences, or is authenticity only recognized by people from that culture? What specific elements (body movement, gestural rhythm, facial expressions, clothing interaction) carry the most cultural information and which can be simplified without losing authenticity? Through documenting the process of translating photogrammetry reference into stylized animation-ready assets, I will establish whether investing in culturally specific asset development addresses animation's credibility problem with audiences, with findings applicable to animation production in emerging markets globally.

Proposal 3: Modular Production Workflow for Small Animation Teams

What I am proposing to do:

Through supervising undergraduate animation projects at KNUST, I observed that students struggle to translate individual technical skills into collaborative production workflows. They can operate software but don't understand how to structure asset creation, manage version control, or coordinate work across team members. Ghana's emerging animation industry faces the same challenge - most productions are solo efforts or small teams without established collaborative methodologies.

I will create a 3-5 minute animated film while deliberately implementing and testing a modular production approach designed for small teams with limited resources. This workflow will emphasize reusable asset libraries, parallel production pipelines where multiple animators can work simultaneously without blocking each other, and clear handoff protocols between production stages. I will work with other MA students at IADT on specific sequences, documenting how the workflow functions in actual collaborative conditions with people who have different technical approaches and working styles. Throughout production, I will maintain detailed logs of what works, what breaks down, where the workflow needs adjustment, and how decisions get made when creative vision conflicts with production constraints.

What I hope to learn:

Which workflow structures allow small teams in resource-constrained contexts to work efficiently? How do you design asset systems that reduce dependency on expensive software licenses or high-end hardware? What's the minimum documentation needed for effective collaboration when team members may be working remotely or part-time? Where do resource-constrained workflows fundamentally differ from large studio pipelines versus just being scaled-down versions? Through creating this film and documenting the production process, I will develop practical workflow frameworks tested in actual production that Ghanaian animation educators and small studios can immediately implement.

Photogrammetry Digital Characters

1/5

2025 | Agisoft Metashape, Wrap3D, Cinema 4D, Blender, DAZ Studio

A photogrammetry-based system for creating authentic Ghanaian 3D characters. I document volunteers from diverse backgrounds, generating production-ready assets for animation and interactive media.

This process demonstrates the complete pipeline using Delali from Ghana's Ewe tribe as a case study. From 360° photo capture through Metashape processing, retopology, and DAZ Studio integration, each step transforms raw photographic data into an animation-ready character while maintaining cultural authenticity and accurate proportions.

Characters created using photogrammetry foundation with stylistic variations developed in DAZ Studio. This library currently covers several ethnic backgrounds in Ghana. The goal is to create as many characters as possible spanning Ghana's diversity, then expand the methodology across Africa. When animators need authentic African characters, this system provides them.

Contemporary Kente Fashion

2/5

2024-2025 | Marvelous Designer, Cinema 4D, V-Ray

Digital garments exploring contemporary applications of traditional Ghanaian kente textiles. I collaborated with a fashion design student on the wedding gown concept, while developing the other designs independently.

Kente-embellished wedding dress combining ornamental bodice detailing with mixed traditional and modern fabric. Built in Marvelous Designer and rendered on one of my photogrammetry characters to test how the design works on Ghanaian body proportions.

Traditional dashiki kaftan with knotted pattern details and contemporary interpretation with kente patterns. These pieces explore how ceremonial menswear translates into digital garment simulation and investigate new contexts for traditional textiles within modern silhouettes beyond conventional applications.

The Comeback | PowerPoint Animation

3/5

2025 | Microsoft PowerPoint, Freepik Assets

In Ghana where many creators and students lack access to expensive animation software, I explored whether animation principles could be demonstrated using universally available tools. This project tests PowerPoint's animation capabilities for storytelling.

This animated short follows three F1 cars in a race. The lead car breaks down at the start, restarts multiple times, then overtakes both opponents just before the finish line.

Working within PowerPoint's constraints required creative solutions for timing, camera movement, and visual storytelling. This approach reflects how resourcefulness and understanding core principles often matter more than access to industry-standard software.

Akofena

4/5

2025 | Cinema 4D, V-Ray, Blender, After Effects

As look development lead and co-supervisor for Akofena, I established the lighting and shading pipeline that unified six students' work into a cohesive visual language. I created several characters, performed motion reference and voice work for the antagonist, and developed the compositing approach. The project investigated how pre-colonial Ashanti narratives could be visualized through contemporary CG techniques while building reusable assets for our emerging character library.

I performed the physical acting for the protagonist character, using my body mechanics as reference for the animation. This side-by-side comparison shows my live-action performance next to the final animation, which combined auto physics simulation with manual keyframe animation.

This shows my lighting and compositing process. I developed the lighting setup for each character using multiple light passes in V-Ray, then imported these passes into After Effects to composite the final promotional poster.

These are the characters I created and supervised for the project. Supervising this project meant balancing teaching production fundamentals while maintaining visual consistency across six students' contributions through a unified lighting and compositing approach.

Ancestral Warrior

5/5

2025 | Cinema 4D, V-Ray, After Effects

Exploring cultural identity through dramatic character visualization. This piece combines traditional African warrior elements with contemporary digital storytelling techniques.

The lighting design uses practical fire sources and atmospheric volumetrics to create cinematic drama while honoring spiritual significance. Multiple compositing layers build environmental storytelling, connecting heritage to landscape through golden hour lighting that suggests transformation. The look development focused on authentic materials rendered with contemporary production quality - traditional elements presented as modern assertion of cultural strength rather than historical recreation.
